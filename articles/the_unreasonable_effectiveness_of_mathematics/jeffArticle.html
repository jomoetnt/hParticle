One of the most unexpectedly puzzling questions at the heart of the philosophy of mathematics is why it is so useful when applied to other fields. Even in fields that were originally developed with no practical applications in mind, humans eventually find use through connections no one anticipated. This was originally discussed in the context of theoretical physics, where the connection is more apparent, but has since been extended to the other natural sciences and even social sciences. Eugene Wigner (mathematical physicist and martian) first popularized this notion in a 1959 lecture which was later published as The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Mathematics in the Natural Sciences.
The question seems like it would only be asked by those completely unfamiliar with mathematics, but it is in fact much more profound than it may first seem.

Examples

The earliest example of this phenomenon (and the first cited by Wigner) comes from Isaac Newton’s application of conic sections to celestial mechanics. Ancient Greek mathematicians studied the various shapes that resulted from the intersection of a plane with a cone, noting three cases: ellipses (including circles), parabolas, and hyperbolas. For them, this was just geometry for its own sake. Newton’s familiarity with these topics allowed him to notice the connection between the motion of projectiles on Earth (parabolic) and the motion of planets in space (elliptic), leading to his famous law of universal gravitation.

Other examples illustrate the useful property of predictive power mathematics has in theoretical physics, where extrapolating led to the discovery of new physical phenomena based purely on abstract reasoning. Returning to gravity, one famous instance of this is Albert Einstein’s theory of general relativity, which was built upon non-Euclidean geometry, a field once considered to have no external applications due to the belief that physical space was Euclidean. Not only did this paradigm shift explain previously known issues with Newtonian gravity (such as the perihelion precession of Mercury), but it also predicted the deflection of starlight during a total solar eclipse, an effect that went unnoticed until then and was observed for the first time just a few years later. Additionally, it predicted the existence of gravitational waves, which were hitherto unobserved and detected for the first time almost exactly 100 years later.
Astronomical Returns
Even when accounting for the gravitational effect of other planets, it was noticed that Mercury’s point of closest approach (called its perihelion) does not change as expected after each revolution. This led some scientists to hypothesize that there was an undiscovered planet they were not including in their calculations, but after no such planet was found, and corrections from general relativity were able to explain the deviation, the idea was abandoned.

Explanations

The most well-known response to Wigner comes from Richard Hamming, a mathematician and early computer scientist. In his essay (which I highly recommend reading), he offers several partial explanations to the question, though in his own humble words, “when all my explanations are over, the residue is still so large as to leave the question essentially unanswered”. The first is simply stated as “we see what we look for”, or in other words, scientists think about the world mathematically, so of course they should find mathematics in it. The second, “we select the kind of mathematics to use”, says that we must tailor mathematics to each specific application, and thus the same mathematics does not work everywhere. The third essentially claims that although mathematics is very effective in the sciences, the sciences are not very effective in answering most questions relating to the human experience (e.g. ethics), so mathematics may not be as effective as it seems. Finally, Hamming claims that there was selective pressure for humans to have mental models closely matching reality, and since mathematics is a mental tool, evolution was a driving force for the effectiveness of mathematics in the sciences.
Other responses (from Michael Atiyah, for example) raise the converse idea; that is, much of mathematics is developed with inspiration from the real world, including physics. Another early example of this comes from Newton’s treatment of kinematics, which made it necessary for him to invent calculus. Later, when Paul Dirac angered the mathematical community by introducing the questionable Dirac delta function for quantum mechanics, it was eventually made rigorous with the theory of distributions. However, the fact that the relationship between physics and mathematics is bidirectional also supports the mathematical universe hypothesis proposed by cosmologist Max Tegmark, which posits that the universe is a mathematical object. This idea has actually been discussed since antiquity, beginning with Pythagoreanism, and can be described as a form of Platonic realism. If it were true, this would easily explain the unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics in physics.

A Word of Caution

We have now seen some of the perspectives on the surprising power of mathematics in other areas of study. We have even seen the power of other areas of study in mathematics. The final point to note is that mathematics often falls short of being unreasonably effective in fields outside of physics, which has sometimes been called the unreasonable ineffectiveness of mathematics. There is a noticeable trend of increasing attempts to apply mathematics in non-scientific or borderline scientific settings that have not been greatly successful. For example, utilitarianism sought to reduce normative ethics to a game of numbers, but fell short of being widely accepted by philosophers. Additionally, mathematical models for finance were heavily invested in, but have since been blamed for causing recessions. It is therefore important to be wary of approaches that claim to produce the objective benefits of mathematics in fields that have historically not been amenable to numerical approaches. This caution should even further extend to accepting physical models without significant supporting experimental evidence, including proposed theories of everything (such as string theory).
